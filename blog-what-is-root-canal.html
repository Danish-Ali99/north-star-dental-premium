What Is a Root Canal Treatment?

Dr. Vartika Kumari · 23 April 2026

Root canals have a scarier reputation than they deserve. Here’s a calm, plain-English look at what actually happens — and why it saves teeth.

If you’ve been told you need a root canal, you may feel a flicker of dread — but it’s a common, well-practised procedure that saves your natural tooth and relieves pain. Let’s demystify exactly what it involves.

What is a root canal?

A root canal treats infection or damage inside a tooth. Beneath the hard outer layers sits the pulp — soft tissue containing the tooth’s nerves and blood vessels. When the pulp becomes infected or inflamed — through a deep cavity, a crack, or an injury — it needs to be removed. The procedure involves taking out the infected pulp, cleaning and disinfecting the inside of the tooth, and filling and sealing the space to prevent further problems. The tooth itself stays; only the damaged inner tissue is removed.

Why would you need one?

The usual reasons are a deep cavity that has reached the pulp, or a crack, chip, or knock that has exposed or damaged it. Once the pulp is infected, the body can’t clear it on its own. Left untreated, the infection can cause significant pain and swelling, form an abscess, spread to surrounding bone, and ultimately lead to losing the tooth. A root canal stops that progression and prevents more serious issues — while keeping the tooth you already have.

What to expect, step by step

Knowing the sequence takes most of the worry out of it:

  1. Numbing. A local anaesthetic is applied so the area is completely numb and you’re comfortable throughout.
  2. Cleaning. A small opening is made in the top of the tooth to access the pulp. The infected tissue is removed, and the canals are gently cleaned, disinfected, and shaped ready to be filled.
  3. Filling and sealing. The cleaned canals are filled with a biocompatible material and sealed. Sometimes a temporary filling is placed first, with the permanent restoration following at a later visit.
  4. Restoration. A crown (or, for some front teeth, a strong filling) is placed to restore the tooth’s strength, function, and appearance — so it looks and works like any other tooth.

Is it painful, and what about recovery?

Thanks to modern anaesthesia and techniques, the procedure itself feels much like having a filling, and many people feel relief afterwards because the source of pain is gone. Mild tenderness for a few days is normal and settles with simple pain relief. Most people return to their normal day straight away.

That’s the whole picture: a routine treatment whose real job is to relieve pain and keep your natural tooth in place for years to come.
