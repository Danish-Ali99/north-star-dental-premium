Caring for Your Tooth After a Root Canal

Dr. Vartika Kumari · 8 May 2026

The treatment is done — here’s how to help your tooth settle and heal smoothly in the days and weeks that follow.

A root canal has an excellent track record, and recovery is usually smooth. A little knowledge about what’s normal — and what isn’t — helps you heal comfortably and protect the tooth for the long term.

The first few days

It’s normal to feel some mild soreness or sensitivity in the treated tooth, particularly when chewing or when pressure is applied. This is the area settling down, and it generally eases within a few days. Over-the-counter pain relief is usually all that’s needed.

While the tooth heals, favour the other side when you eat and avoid hard, chewy, or sticky foods that could stress or dislodge the temporary restoration. Keep up gentle but regular brushing and flossing around the area to keep it clean — good hygiene is the best defence against re-infection. A follow-up appointment lets us confirm the tooth has healed properly and place the permanent restoration if it’s not already done.

Possible side effects

Most people sail through recovery, but it helps to know the full range of what can occasionally happen:

  • Pain or sensitivity — usually temporary, easing over a few days.
  • Swelling and inflammation in the gum or jaw near the tooth.
  • Infection — uncommon, but possible if bacteria remain or re-enter.
  • Tooth discolouration developing gradually over time.
  • Brittleness — a root canal-treated tooth can become more prone to cracking if it isn’t protected with a proper restoration such as a crown.
  • Allergic reactions to materials used during the procedure (rare).
  • Prolonged pain, which can occasionally signal incomplete treatment.
  • Nerve sensitivity — very rare.

How to manage it

Simple steps cover most situations: take pain relief as directed; apply a cold compress to the outside of the cheek to ease swelling; rinse gently with warm salt water to soothe the gum and keep it clean; finish any antibiotics exactly as prescribed; and protect the tooth with softer foods until it’s fully restored with its permanent filling or crown. Above all, don’t skip the follow-up — the crown is what gives the tooth its long-term strength.

When to seek care

Get in touch with us if you experience pain that persists beyond about a week, visible swelling with pus, a fever, or ongoing difficulty chewing. These are uncommon and almost always easy to address — and we would always rather see you sooner than later.

Questions After Your Treatment?
